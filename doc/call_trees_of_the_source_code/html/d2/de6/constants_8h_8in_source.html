143 ! For the reference ellipsoid to convert geographic latitudes to geocentric:
144 !
145 ! From Dahlen and Tromp (1998): "Spherically-symmetric Earth models all have the same hydrostatic
146 ! surface ellipticity 1/299.8. This is 0.5 percent smaller than observed flattening of best-fitting ellipsoid 1/298.3.
147 ! The discrepancy is referred to as the "excess equatorial bulge of the Earth",
148 ! an early discovery of artificial satellite geodesy."
149 !
150 ! From Paul Melchior, IUGG General Assembly, Vienna, Austria, August 1991 Union lecture,
151 ! available at http://www.agu.org/books/sp/v035/SP035p0047/SP035p0047.pdf :
152 ! "It turns out that the spheroidal models constructed on the basis of the spherically-symmetric models (PREM, 1066A)
153 ! by using the Clairaut differential equation to calculate the flattening in function of the radius vector imply hydrostaticity.
154 ! These have surface ellipticity 1/299.8 and a corresponding dynamical flattening of .0033 (PREM).
155 ! The actual ellipticty of the Earth for a best-fitting ellipsoid is 1/298.3 with a corresponding dynamical flattening of .0034."
156 !
157 ! Thus, flattening f = 1/299.8 is what is used in SPECFEM3D_GLOBE, as it should.
158 ! And thus eccentricity squared e^2 = 1 - (1-f)^2 = 1 - (1 - 1/299.8)^2 = 0.00665998813529,
159 ! and the correction factor used in the code to convert geographic latitudes to geocentric
160 ! is 1 - e^2 = (1-f)^2 = (1 - 1/299.8)^2 = 0.9933400118647.
161 !
162 ! As a comparison, the classical World Geodetic System reference ellipsoid WGS 84
163 ! (see e.g. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Geodetic_System) has f = 1/298.2572236.
164  double precision, parameter :: FLATTENING_F = 1.d0 / 299.8d0
165  double precision, parameter :: ONE_MINUS_F_SQUARED = (1.d0 - FLATTENING_F)**2
